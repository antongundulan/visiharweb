It is possible to do a basic test while the AVR is still installed on the AC generator. Turn the voltage trim pot up and down to observe what happens to the output of the alternating current generator. You may also bench test any AVR by using a variable output Autotransformer (variac) and a test load that replicates the resistance of the alternating current generator field.

Also, what happens if the AVR does not work?

As soon as the AVR fails, a safety feature known as Field Failure Protection will kick in and cause the generator to trip. Failure of field is related with low voltage, which may occur as a result of a severe failure near the generator and the AVR may trip as a result of being unable to sustain voltage, in which case the generator is tripped instantly.

My generator also produces high voltage, which raises the issue of why.

Usually, when the generator produces high voltage, it is because the engine is operating at an excessively rapid speed. It is possible that anything that is hanging or dragging will cause the engine to run excessively quickly.

What is causing my generator to produce such low voltage?

Capacitor. There are two roles performed by the capacitor: it induces voltage into the rotor and it controls voltage. Because the power supplied by the generator will be derived from the residual magnetism of the rotor, a low voltage reading from the generator will result from a low capacity (usually about 2-5V). If this is not the case, the capacitor must be changed.
